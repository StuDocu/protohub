The starting point

Where students actually study

Before they ever see our page, students already have a stack of tools. Every session we lose, we lose to one of these.

NotebookLM ChatGPT Claude YouTube Textbooks Paper notes Past papers Their own notes Lecturer + tutor Mind maps

Study happens everywhere but Studocu. The moment we miss on source, structure, or accuracy, the session leaves.

01
Act one · What landed

The hub lands

The win is not any single artifact. It is having topics, deadlines, documents, quizzes, summaries, recordings and practice in one place, framed around the exam. All six students read it that way, and most jumped straight into the Today quiz or started browsing the tabs.

6/6 aggregation is the value 6/6 topic structure understood 6/6 exam urgency survived

Exam countdown up top, everything else one tap away. Tap the shot to open the live prototype.

"I genuinely think so, because everything is organized really nicely."

P1, industrial engineering student
02
Act two · Needs reinforcement

Tomorrow half-lands

Good news first: five of six read the today and tomorrow framing on their own. They just read it as a schedule, not as memory. The reason for the wait, that spacing it out is what makes it stick, never came through. Two even asked to keep going now.

5/6 got the today/tomorrow framing read as a plan, not memory docs valued, volume doubted

A plan, not a mechanic

Students followed the structure. What did not land is the point of it: that spacing is what makes it stick. This needs reinforcement, not a rebuild.

Legible as a plan, not as a memory mechanic. The doc shelf is rich, but volume and labels raised doubt.

"So it's like the topic that I can study tomorrow. But I can also study them now if I want, if I have more time."

P6, recent international business graduate

What we're trying next

A hypothesis, not a finding. Tomorrow half-lands because it looks like leftovers. So make it look like more.

  • Mix the lanes. Keep today's question card as the hero, then add an "also today" lane of mixed artifacts under it: documents, videos, summaries. The before-midterm shelf is already this pattern.
  • Make Tomorrow a mix too, not just review. If tomorrow visibly holds new material, "tomorrow I get more" pulls students back instead of feeling gated.
  • Tie the beat to the countdown. Every day you show up, readiness climbs toward the target. By exam week there is nothing left to cram. That is the one thing cramming cannot offer.

Two things we still owe

One plain sentence on why the wait helps, or students infer nothing about memory. And a display answer: we do not yet know how mixed artifact tiles should sit inside the focused question rhythm.

03
Act three · The gate

The payoff needs teeth

The readiness check is a great low-effort start. What happens after a wrong answer decides everything. Students wanted a score, an explanation, a source link, a way to strengthen the weak topic. Not more questions. And under all of it sits trust.

6/6 trust is the adoption gate score + remediation wanted

Trust is the real blocker

Flagged by all six. For calculation-heavy courses it is severe: one engineering student left SAI because she could not trust the steps. Ungrounded "most likely to be tested" claims drew the same distrust across the board.

The result screen students responded to: a score, momentum, and Strengthen this topic. This is the shape to build on.

"I could only rely on the theoretical part of the exam. For the calculation part, I couldn't actually trust it."

P2, engineering student
04
Act four · Where next

Where they want to go next

Two formats came up again and again, unprompted: short video and podcast. For studying on the go, when tired, when they do not want a screen. Meanwhile the "infinite" in infinite practice backfired. Endless reads as overwhelming.

video + podcast: unprompted (4/6) "infinite" overwhelms

The video and podcast cards land. The infinity card is the one to rethink.

Kill the word "infinite"

A bounded, scored session motivates more than endlessness. Give practice a finish line: score, weak areas, and a clear next step.

"It generated this video and I found it super funny, and it made me actually want to listen."

P1, on auto-generated video

Bonus · Exam urgency

Cram mode

Exam urgency was one of the strongest signals, all six felt it. But the calm page underplayed it. One student said his midterm did not feel important enough to act on. The answer is escalation: when the exam is close, the page stops being calm.

6/6 exam urgency survived the calm page underplayed it

Under two days out: red countdown to the hour, a readiness bar against a target, the loop reordered to highest-impact-first, and rapid recall for the facts most likely to be tested.

"When I look at here, it doesn't feel like my midterm is important."

P5, on the calm page. On the exam-forward version: "This is what I'm looking for."
3
The takeaway

Three fixes before we bet on the loop

The hub concept is validated. Before we invest more in the daily loop, three fixes carry the most weight.

What to fix first

FIX 1 Ground the claims. Sources, topic coverage, calculation reliability. Trust is the gate on adoption.
FIX 2 Build the payoff. After a wrong answer: score, explanation, source, strengthen. Not more questions.
FIX 3 Reinforce spacing. Mix Tomorrow's lanes so it reads as more, not leftovers, add one line on why the wait helps, and give practice a finish line.

Read it with the caveats

Six sessions, run on mock data rather than students' real courses. Retention is stated intent only, no return behavior was observed. Confidence: medium. Strong signals: aggregation, trust, and the spacing miss.

Now tell me where I'm wrong.

Six sessions is a strong signal, not gospel. Push on it, then let's decide what to build.
